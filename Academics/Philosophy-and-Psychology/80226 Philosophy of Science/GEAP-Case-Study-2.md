## SCI1: Question 1

The shift from catastrophism to uniformitarianism is honestly a really clean illustration of what Kuhn means by a paradigm shift. Catastrophists like Cuvier had a perfectly workable framework for explaining the fossil record, and it was not like they were ignoring evidence. The problem was that they were working with a deeply compressed sense of time, assuming centuries where there were actually millions of years. Once Hutton introduced the concept of deep time, and Lyell systematized it in the Principles of Geology, that compressed timeline collapsed, and with it the need for catastrophe as an explanatory tool. Ordinary processes like erosion and sedimentation, given enough time, could do everything a catastrophe supposedly did. The key drivers of this change were the accumulation of geological evidence that resisted catastrophist interpretation, the growing acceptance of deep time as a legitimate scientific concept, and Lyell's methodological insistence that scientific uncertainty should prompt further investigation rather than appeals to supernatural forces. What this reveals about scientific inquiry more broadly is that paradigm shifts are rarely triggered by one decisive experiment, they are driven by the slow erosion of an old framework's coherence and the emergence of a more elegant alternative, which is exactly what Kuhn was trying to show us.

## SCI2: Question 2

Darwin's approach to scientific inquiry is genuinely impressive and it holds up even when you read it through a pretty rigorous philosophical lens. In terms of data collection, Darwin was meticulous to a degree that bordered on obsessive, he spent years on the Beagle voyage accumulating observations across geology, biology, and geography, and then spent another twenty-plus years after returning to England gathering more evidence before publishing. He drew from artificial selection in domestic breeding, the geographical distribution of species, the fossil record, comparative anatomy, and embryology, pulling together what Whewell would call a consilience of inductions, where independent lines of evidence all converge on the same explanatory framework. Darwin was constructing a mechanism, the argument he built was structurally tight: variation exists within populations, some variation is heritable, more offspring are produced than can survive, therefore individuals with advantageous traits will differentially survive and reproduce. What makes his theory-building especially sophisticated is that he also explicitly engaged with the hardest objections to his own framework, like the complexity of the eye, and showed how gradual accumulation through selection could produce it. That kind of critical self-assessment anticipates Popper's falsifiability criterion in a way that most scientists of his era did not bother with, and it is one of the things that makes Darwin's approach feel genuinely modern even now.

## SCI3: Question 3

Looking at both the Copernican and Darwinian revolutions, I think the most important takeaway for contemporary scientists is that the social reception of a discovery is a predictable feature of what happens when science fundamentally displaces human beings from a position of centrality or specialness. Copernicus removed the Earth from the center of the cosmos, and the institutional and religious backlash was enormous because the cosmological picture was not just a scientific claim, it was a theological and philosophical one. Darwin did something structurally similar when he removed human beings from a position of divine creation and placed them inside the same history of descent and struggle as every other organism, and the societal resistance followed the same pattern. Both cases demonstrate that scientific discoveries land in communities with existing metaphysical commitments, institutional interests, and deep emotional investments in particular pictures of the world. What Galileo's confrontation with the Church already showed, and what Darwin's reception confirmed, is that the more a discovery touches on questions of human meaning and cosmic order, the harder the diffusion process becomes. Contemporary scientists should take seriously that clear, patient public communication is not optional, and that being scientifically correct is not sufficient to make a discovery culturally legible. Understanding the sociology of knowledge, as Kuhn would put it, is part of the scientific responsibility.
