# Week 12.2 Question 3

## Question Restatement

The question asks whether Planck's assumption that energy is exchanged only in discrete units is inconsistent with Newton's laws of physics themselves, or only with the background assumption that physical quantities like mass and velocity can take on any continuous real number value, or both.
## Argument

The most defensible initial reading is that Planck's quantization assumption is primarily inconsistent with the continuity assumption rather than with Newton's laws as such. Newton's laws are fundamentally relational. The second law says that force equals mass times acceleration, and the third law says that for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. What these laws do is specify how physical quantities relate to one another, but they are technically silent on whether those quantities must vary continuously. In principle, you could imagine a world in which mass can only take on whole number values in kilograms, velocity only whole number values in meters per second, and so on, and Newton's laws would still describe how those discretized quantities relate to each other. The structure of the law survives even if the domain over which it operates has been restricted. The analogy in the question makes this point well: if an object with 10.4 joules of kinetic energy can only transfer 10 joules on impact, that is strange and counterintuitive, but it is the assumption about what values energy can take that breaks, not the relationship F = ma itself. Planck's quantum condition is, on this reading, a rupture in the metaphysics underlying classical physics, specifically the assumption of continuity inherited from the infinitesimal calculus, rather than a direct violation of any law in Newton's three.


## Counterargument

This distinction is more appealing than it is sound, and the counterargument is the more interesting move. Newton's laws are not freestanding logical structures that can be transplanted into arbitrary domains. They are inseparable from the mathematical framework in which they were formulated, and that framework is the calculus, which is built entirely on the assumption of continuity. Instantaneous velocity is defined as a derivative, which presupposes a continuous function. Acceleration is the second derivative. Force in Newton's framework is not just algebraically proportional to acceleration, it is the continuous rate of change of momentum with respect to time. None of these concepts even make sense in a world where quantities jump between discrete values rather than varying smoothly. If you try to apply Newton's second law in a fully discretized setting, you lose the mathematical tools that give the law its meaning and predictive power in the first place. The calculus and the laws are so thoroughly entangled that isolating one from the other is a philosophical fiction. Maxwell's equations face the same problem. They describe electromagnetic fields as continuous functions across space and time, and the oscillator picture that Planck inherited from Maxwell assumes that energy radiates in a smooth, continuous spectrum. So when Planck introduced the quantum condition, he was not just quietly adjusting a background assumption about numerical domains while leaving the laws untouched. He was undermining the entire mathematical scaffolding on which those laws depend. The continuity assumption is not an optional add-on to classical physics that can be removed without consequence; it is constitutive of what classical physics actually is. That is precisely why Planck himself described the move as an act of desperation rather than a routine refinement. He understood that he was not simply modifying an ancillary premise. He was cutting against the grain of the whole framework, which is what makes the quantum assumption so radical and what makes the question of its consistency with classical physics something more than a technicality about number domains.
