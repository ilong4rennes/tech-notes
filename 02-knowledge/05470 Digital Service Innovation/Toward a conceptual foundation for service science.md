- The paper argues for shifting from a **goods-dominant (G-D) logic** to a **service-dominant (S-D) logic** as the conceptual foundation for service science.
- **Service definition**: applying competences (skills, knowledge, resources) for the benefit of another party.
- **Goods-dominant logic**: focuses on efficiency, standardization, production, and delivering value embedded in tangible products.
- **Service-dominant logic**: focuses on effectiveness, co-creation of value, ongoing processes, and experiences.
- **Resources**
    - Operand (passive): need to be acted upon (land, raw materials).
    - Operant (active): can act on others (knowledge, skills).
    - Modern value creation relies more on operant resources.

- **Resourcing processes**
    - Resource creation: transforming something into usable form (oil from nuisance to energy).
    - Resource integration: combining internal and external resources (Dell assembling, IBM modular systems).
    - Removing resistance: eliminating barriers to resource use (reducing bureaucracy at HP).

- **Servicing and experience**
    - Focus on usefulness and customer experience before efficiency.
    - Example: empower frontline staff to resolve problems instead of passing customers around.
        
- **Value propositions and co-creation**
    - Firms cannot deliver value directly, only offer value propositions.
    - Customers realize value by integrating the offering with their own resources.
    - Value is dynamic: benefits and costs accrue over time.
        
- **Dialogue**
    - Replace one-way promotion with two-way or multi-way dialogue.
    - Internet enables communities and many-to-many exchanges.
    - Trust and shared learning are key.
        
- **Value networks**
    - Move from supply chains to value-creation networks.
    - Key question: how to mobilize, connect, and reconfigure capabilities.
    - Information can move independently of goods (“liquefaction”), enabling new service models.
    - Example: Nike focusing on design/brand, outsourcing manufacturing, and engaging customers in brand co-creation.
        
- **Exchange as learning**
    - Exchange is not just transferring goods/money, but testing assumptions and learning.
    - Cash flow acts as a feedback mechanism on whether resource integration and value co-creation work.
        
- **Collaborative marketing**
    - From “marketing to” customers to “marketing with” customers
    - Customers seen as partners in innovation and delivery.
    - Outsourcing, alliances, and partnerships reflect this shift.
        
- **Managerial implications**
    - Define business in terms of service and experience, not product units.
    - Invest in knowledge, skills, and organizational learning.
    - Build mechanisms for co-creation with customers.
    - View markets as dynamic networks, not linear chains.
    - Replace push advertising with ongoing dialogue.
    - Treat every exchange as an opportunity to learn and adapt.
    - Continuously remove organizational, technical, and cultural barriers.

## UX vs Service Design

- **Main idea**: UX Design and Service Design are related but different in scope — UX is the “tip of the iceberg,” while Service Design is the whole iceberg.
    
- **UX Design**
    - Focuses on the user’s experience at specific interaction points (touchpoints).
    - Examples: checking train times online, buying a ticket, looking at station screens.
    - Mainly concerned with digital and individual moments in a journey.
    - Typical outputs: wireframes, personas, prototypes.
        
- **Service Design**
    - Broader, covering the entire end-to-end system.
    - Includes physical infrastructure, staff processes, and technology integration.
    - Examples: bus design for accessibility, scheduling train frequency, integrating data with Google Maps, payment methods, energy sources.
    - Typical outputs: service blueprints, customer journey maps, service ecology maps.
        
- **Overlap and boundaries**
    - Both use research methods like interviews, workshops, prototypes.
    - UX = detailed focus on touchpoints.
    - Service Design = systemic perspective, coordinating multiple touchpoints and resources.
        
- **Good Service Design**
    - Balances user needs, business goals, ethics, and sustainability.
    - Real-world trade-offs are common (e.g., adopting cheaper ticketing tech but losing a valued feature).
    - Poor service design ignores these balances.
        
- **Conclusion**
    - UX and Service Design overlap but differ in scale.
    - Service Design’s strength is mapping problems at the organizational/system level.
    - A mindset of zooming in (user detail) and zooming out (system design) prepares organizations for growing and complex demands.
